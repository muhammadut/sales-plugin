# Blount — Objections

## One-line summary
Blount's contribution to objection handling is the **RBO classification** — Reflexive Brush-Offs (autopilot dismissals like "I'm not interested"), True Objections (actual concerns about fit), and Buying Conditions (negotiation-stage friction) — and the "ledge" technique for buying time mid-objection without sounding rattled.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. As with Fanatical Prospecting, Blount writes from career sales-trainer experience, not controlled research. There are no studies behind the RBO categories. What earns it scaffolding-tier is that the **classification itself is a forcing function**: a seller who categorizes every incoming objection into Reflexive vs True vs Buying Condition will respond very differently, and most untrained sellers treat all three the same way (defending, explaining, or panicking). The "ledge" technique — buying 2-3 seconds with a deliberate phrase like "That's exactly why I called" — gives the seller cognitive room to NOT respond reflexively, which is where most calls die. The classification is the value; whether the three categories are mutually exclusive in some deep sense is irrelevant.

## Key concepts
- **Reflexive Brush-Off (RBO).** An autopilot dismissal that has nothing to do with the actual product or fit: "Not interested," "Send me an email," "Just put me on the do-not-call list." These are the prospect's defensive autopilot — they happened before the prospect even processed who was calling. The right response is NOT to defend; it is to acknowledge, redirect, and earn 30 more seconds.
- **True Objection.** A real concern about fit, price, timing, authority, or trust. "We already use HomeAdvisor and they're terrible" is a True Objection — there's a real comparison happening. The right response is to surface the concern fully (Voss-style mirroring) before answering it.
- **Buying Condition.** A late-stage stipulation in a near-sale conversation: "We need a trial," "We can't sign without legal review." Not actually objections — they are the prospect telling you what it would take to close. Treat them as roadmap, not resistance.
- **The Ledge.** A deliberate stalling phrase that gives the seller 2-3 seconds to think without sounding caught off guard. Examples: "That's exactly why I called," "I hear that a lot," "That makes sense — and the reason I'm calling is..." The ledge is not the answer; it is the pause before the answer.
- **The Three Why's.** Before responding to any objection, ask yourself three internal questions: Why is the prospect saying this? What do they actually want? What is the smallest next step I could ask for?
- **Disrupt the pattern.** RBOs are autopilot, so the seller's response should also disrupt autopilot. Saying something unexpected ("You're right, you should hang up — except you'll wonder for the rest of the day what I was about to say") breaks the script the prospect is running.

1. "The single biggest reason that prospecting fails is that salespeople give up at the first sign of resistance. Most objections are reflexive, not real." — paraphrase of *Objections*, 2018, Chapter 1.

2. "A Reflexive Brush-Off is what the prospect says before they've even finished processing who you are. It is not a position; it is a reflex. Treat it like a reflex and you will lose the call. Disrupt the reflex and you earn the next 30 seconds." — paraphrase of Chapter 4 framing.

3. "The Ledge is the verbal equivalent of taking a breath. It buys you the second you need to choose a thoughtful response instead of a defensive one." — paraphrase of Chapter 6 framing on the Ledge technique.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Blount's RBO categories as if they were validated psychological constructs — they are a useful sorting heuristic, not a theory of buyer cognition. Do not let `script-writer` write a "response per objection" library that ignores the category — the entire point of citing Blount is that the response strategy differs by category. For the actual response language, pair Blount's classification with Voss's mirroring/labeling techniques; Blount tells you what kind of objection it is, Voss tells you what to say next.
