# Braun — Poke the Bear

## One-line summary
Josh Braun's playbook is the modern anti-pitch school of cold outreach: open with a problem the buyer probably has (the "poke"), explicitly disclaim the pitch ("I'm not sure if this is even a fit"), and let the buyer pull you forward instead of pushing them backward.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. Braun is a sales trainer (Sales DNA, Bad Decisions Founder Coaching) running a popular newsletter and YouTube channel. There are no controlled studies behind the playbook — what exists is a large body of paid training content, public LinkedIn posts, and self-reported student wins. What earns it scaffolding-tier is the **anti-pitch reframe is a forcing function**: it makes the seller name a specific buyer problem and resist the urge to immediately propose a solution. The "permission opener" and "poke the bear" patterns are tactically useful even if the underlying behavioral claim ("buyers buy when they don't feel sold to") is folk psychology rather than experimental finding. For founders selling into trades — where the buyer's defensive posture is the default — the disarming pattern works.

## Key concepts
- **The Permission Opener.** "Hey [name], I know I'm calling out of the blue — can I take 27 seconds to tell you why I called and you can decide if it makes sense to keep talking?" The 27 seconds is specific (not "a minute") and the buyer's autonomy is named explicitly.
- **Poke the Bear.** Lead with a problem the buyer probably has, not your solution. "I notice a lot of [vertical] owners get a flood of calls Tuesday morning and then it's dead Wednesday — does that match your week?" The "poke" is a real problem; the buyer either confirms or denies.
- **No-pitch CTA.** Do not ask for a meeting on the first call. Ask a question that earns the right to a follow-up: "Would it make sense if I sent you a one-pager so you can decide?" The CTA is information, not commitment.
- **Reframe objections as opinions.** When the buyer says "we already use X" or "send me an email," do not push back. Mirror it ("you already use X...") and let them elaborate. The objection is not the answer.
- **Subject lines: short and human.** "quick question," "{first-name}," "is this a fit?" Avoid corporate subject lines that get filtered.
- **Email body: 3-5 sentences, problem-first.** No "I hope this email finds you well." Sentence 1 is the problem the buyer probably has; sentence 2 is one piece of relevant proof; sentence 3 is the soft CTA.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Josh Braun for any specific numerical claim (e.g., "Permission Openers convert 2x better"). The numbers floating around his content are not from controlled studies. Do not let `script-writer` use BOTH Braun's Permission Opener and Blount's 5-step framework in the same script — they are different shapes and produce different call rhythms; pick one. Do not cite Braun as "research"; cite him as a contemporary playbook with a coherent anti-pitch stance.
