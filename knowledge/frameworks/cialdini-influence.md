# Cialdini — Influence

## One-line summary
Cialdini's six (now seven) principles of persuasion — Reciprocity, Commitment/Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, Scarcity, and Unity — emerged from his 35 years of academic social-psychology research at Arizona State and from a three-year participant-observer field study inside compliance professions (sales, fundraising, advertising), and most have been replicated thousands of times in independent experiments.

## Why this tier
Evidence, with one of the strongest provenance chains in any sales-adjacent book. Cialdini is a published academic social psychologist (Regents' Professor Emeritus at Arizona State University) and the principles in *Influence* are drawn from controlled laboratory experiments and field studies in the peer-reviewed social-psychology literature. The original six principles each have hundreds of replications in independent labs; the underlying mechanisms (reciprocity as a near-universal human norm, social proof in ambiguous situations, authority compliance from Milgram's work) are some of the most robust findings in social psychology. Where Cialdini does cite weaker evidence (some of the Liking and Unity examples) he is explicit about the strength of the source. The book's status as the load-bearing text of behavioral influence is earned. **Caveat:** the Cialdini-trained "use these triggers in your sales pitch" school often degrades into manipulation rather than persuasion; the evidence is for the principles, not for any specific scripted application.

## Key concepts
- **Reciprocity.** People feel a strong obligation to return favors, gifts, or concessions. In sales: give first (free advice, a useful link, a free sample call recording) before asking. The reciprocity reflex is automatic and culturally near-universal.
- **Commitment and Consistency.** Once people make a small commitment, they feel pressure to act consistently with it. In sales: get a small "yes" early (waitlist signup, free trial, agreement on a small statement) before asking for the big "yes."
- **Social Proof.** In ambiguous situations, people look to what similar others are doing. In sales: "Other plumbers in Hamilton are doing X" beats "You should do X." Strongest when the reference group is similar to the prospect.
- **Authority.** People defer to perceived experts and authority symbols. In sales: credentials, named clients, published work, recognizable affiliations. Authority cues should be true (false authority gets caught and destroys trust).
- **Liking.** People say yes more often to people they like — and they like people who are similar to them, who compliment them sincerely, and who share goals with them. In sales: find genuine common ground, do not fake it.
- **Scarcity.** People value things more when they are rare or time-limited. In sales: "limited founding-member spots" works IF the scarcity is real. Fake scarcity is the most-detected manipulation.
- **Unity (added in the 2016 revision).** Beyond liking — people are persuaded most by those they consider "one of us." Family, tribe, neighborhood, profession. The strongest persuasion comes from shared identity.

## When to cite
Cited by `script-writer` whenever a script element invokes one of the seven principles — every "founding-member" framing cites Scarcity AND Commitment, every "other plumbers in Hamilton" framing cites Social Proof AND Unity, every free-call-recording offer cites Reciprocity. Cited by `cadence-designer` when the cadence includes a "give-first" touch (e.g., a free useful link in email 2 before asking for anything in email 3). Cited by `call-coach` when grading whether the user invoked a relevant principle at the right moment. Cited by `sales-researcher` as the strongest behavioral-science backing in the bundle.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Cialdini to justify any **fake** version of a principle — false scarcity ("only 3 spots left" when there are 300), fake authority, manufactured social proof. Cialdini himself warns these get detected and destroy trust permanently. Do not cite Cialdini's principles as "tricks" — frame them as ethical defaults the user should know they are using. The 2016 *Pre-Suasion* book is a separate Cialdini work focused on framing/timing of influence; if the founder asks about it, note that it is a complementary work but not in this bundle.
