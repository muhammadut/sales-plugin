# Dixon and Adamson — The Challenger Sale

## One-line summary
Dixon and Adamson surveyed 6,000 sales reps for the CEB (now Gartner) and proposed that one of five seller profiles — "the Challenger" — outperforms the others by teaching the customer something new and taking control of the conversation; the segmentation is weak but the "bring commercial insight" advice that survives the segmentation is real.

## Why this tier
Vocabulary-only, with a partial rescue. The five-profile segmentation (Challenger, Hard Worker, Lone Wolf, Reactive Problem Solver, Relationship Builder) is the marketing hook of the book and is **the part that does not survive scrutiny**. It is a survey-based factor analysis of self-reported seller behaviors, not a controlled study, and independent attempts to replicate the "Challengers outperform" finding have produced mixed results. Customer-facing, the five labels function as a Rorschach for the founding team to argue about, not a predictor of who will hit quota. **What is real** is the substantive advice in the second half of the book: in complex B2B sales the seller who walks in with a non-obvious commercial insight ("here is something about your business you don't know") wins more meetings than the seller who walks in asking discovery questions. That advice is real but it does not need a five-profile framework — it needs research and homework. The Teach-Tailor-Take-Control structure is useful as an opener pattern; the seller-segmentation is not.

## Key concepts
- **Teach.** Open the conversation with a non-obvious insight about the buyer's business they did not previously have.
- **Tailor.** Ground the insight in the specific buyer's role, industry, and metrics — generic insight is condescending.
- **Take Control.** Lead the buyer through a structured conversation rather than letting the buyer dictate pace; specifically, do not flinch on price or scope.
- **Commercial Teaching.** The 5-step pitch arc: warmer → reframe → rational drowning → emotional impact → new way → your solution.
- **The five profiles (vocabulary-only).** Challenger, Hard Worker, Lone Wolf, Reactive Problem Solver, Relationship Builder. The book's central claim that Challengers win 54% of complex deals vs 7% for Relationship Builders has not been independently replicated and is not load-bearing for this plugin.

## When to cite
Cited by `script-writer` ONLY when constructing a script that opens with a teaching insight (e.g., "Most Hamilton plumbers I talk to don't realize that 60% of their inbound calls come Tuesday-Thursday — and they staff the same Mon-Fri") — that opener pattern is the part of Challenger that works. Cited by `sales-researcher` only as a counterweight when the founder asks "should I just ask discovery questions?" — sometimes the answer is no, walk in with insight first.

## Anti-citation guidance
**Do not cite the five seller profiles** as a predictor of anything. They are vocabulary, not theory. Do not let the script-writer or call-coach assign the user to a profile and then critique their calls against it. Do not cite "Challengers outperform Relationship Builders 54% to 7%" — that statistic is from the book's original survey, has not been independently replicated, and the methodology (self-reported survey) is too weak to support that strong a claim. Use the Teach-Tailor-Take-Control opener pattern; ignore the profile taxonomy.
