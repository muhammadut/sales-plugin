# Jones — Exactly What to Say

## One-line summary
Phil M. Jones's book is a catalog of 23 short "magic words" — micro-phrases like "Just imagine...", "What makes you say that?", "I'm not sure if it's for you, but..." — each one designed to be dropped verbatim into a conversation at a specific moment to redirect the prospect's thinking with minimum friction.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. Jones is a sales trainer (Phil M. Jones Training Ltd) and the book is a phrase catalog with anecdotal evidence and zero controlled studies. The behavioral claims behind each phrase ("'just imagine' triggers visualization which feels like already having the thing") are folk-cognitive-science, not peer-reviewed findings. **However**, the catalog is a useful scaffolding because the phrases are concrete and copy-pasteable — a founder who memorizes 5-6 of them and drops them at the right moments will outperform a founder who improvises. The book's value is not the theory; it is that it gives the seller a finite vocabulary of high-leverage micro-phrases that work as ledges, transitions, and closes. For a non-natural seller (which the elocal_clone founder explicitly is), having pre-loaded phrases is the difference between freezing and continuing.

## Key concepts
- **"Just imagine..."** Triggers the prospect to mentally pre-experience having your solution. Used right before a CTA to soften it: "Just imagine getting 5 plumbing calls a day next week without having to bid on any of them — that's what we're talking about."
- **"What makes you say that?"** A neutral, non-defensive response to almost any objection. Mirrors Voss's calibrated questions but with simpler grammar. Forces the prospect to elaborate on their own objection, which often dissolves it.
- **"I'm not sure if it's for you, but..."** The takeaway opener. Triggers reactance: as soon as you tell someone something might not be for them, they want to know what it is.
- **"Before you make your mind up..."** Used to forestall a hard "no." Buys you the right to add one more piece of information before the prospect commits.
- **"How would you feel if..."** A future-projection phrase that makes the prospect imagine the negative outcome of doing nothing. The discomfort is generated by the prospect, not the seller.
- **"Most people in your position..."** Social-proof opener. Lets the seller introduce a recommendation without claiming personal authority. "Most plumbers I've talked to in Hamilton tell me..."
- **"Just out of curiosity..."** A disarming prefix that converts a probing question into casual interest. Defuses defensiveness. "Just out of curiosity — how are you handling the slow days right now?"
- **"The good news is..."** Reframes any setback or objection as a positive. Used after acknowledging a concern: "I hear you on HomeAdvisor — the good news is, we don't work like that."

## When to cite
Cited by `script-writer` as the source for the **talk-tracks.md** file — the Phil M. Jones magic words are the bulk of the reusable mid-call phrases the caller can drop in. Each phrase in the talk-tracks file should be tagged with its source (Jones, Voss, etc.) so the user knows what they're saying and why. Cited by `call-coach` when the user fumbles a transition — the coach's first suggestion is usually "you needed a Phil Jones phrase here."

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Jones for any psychological mechanism claim ("'just imagine' activates the visualization cortex") — those mechanisms are folk neuroscience. Cite the phrases as **patterns that work in practice for trained sellers**, not as cognitive levers. Do not let `script-writer` build the entire script out of magic words — the phrases are seasoning, not the meal. The script needs a structural framework (Blount, SPIN, Challenger, Braun) underneath; Jones's phrases drop into the structure.
