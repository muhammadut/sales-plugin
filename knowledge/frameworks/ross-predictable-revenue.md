# Ross and Tyler — Predictable Revenue

## One-line summary
Aaron Ross's account of the outbound machine he built at Salesforce.com that added $100M in recurring revenue is the origin document for the modern SDR/AE role split and for "Cold Calling 2.0" — email-first prospecting against named-account lists rather than dialing through a yellow pages.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. The book is a practitioner case study from one company (Salesforce.com, 2002-2008) and the numerical claims are post-hoc reconstructions, not controlled experiments. What earns it scaffolding-tier is that the **role-split structure** — specialized SDRs who do nothing but prospect, separate from AEs who do nothing but close — has been adopted by essentially every modern B2B SaaS sales org, and it is the structural pattern a solo founder is implicitly choosing whether to copy or reject. The book is the forcing function that makes a founder enumerate their outbound roles ("am I the SDR or am I the closer or am I both?") and pick a sequence pattern for cold prospecting that does not start with the dial. For a 2-person team like elocal_clone, the relevant lesson is not the role split (impossible at 2 people) but the email-first sequence and the named-account list discipline.

## Key concepts
- **Cold Calling 2.0.** Stop dialing strangers from a yellow-pages list. Start with a named-account list, send a short referral email to a senior person asking for the right contact, then call the contact who replies. The dial becomes a warm followup, not a cold opener.
- **Role specialization.** Four roles, not one generalist seller: (1) SDR / Outbound Prospector — does only outbound, (2) MRR / Inbound Qualifier — handles inbound leads, (3) Account Executive — closes qualified opportunities, (4) Customer Success Manager — handles post-sale. Mixing roles destroys throughput.
- **Ideal Customer Profile (ICP).** Before any outreach, write the ICP: industry, employee count, revenue, technographics, geography, named-account list. The ICP is the gating document.
- **150-250 emails per week per SDR.** Volume target for outbound prospecting from the original Salesforce playbook. Useful as a discipline anchor; not a defensible benchmark.
- **The referral email.** Short, no pitch, asks the senior contact for the name of the right person to talk to. The trick is permission and brevity: "Aaron, can you point me to the right person at Acme who handles X?"
- **Trust the process.** Outbound at scale is a math problem: dials × connect rate × conversation rate × meeting rate × close rate = revenue. Optimize each step independently.

## When to cite
Cited by `icp-analyst` whenever defending the discipline of writing the ICP before scraping or sourcing — Predictable Revenue is the canonical reference for ICP-first. Cited by `cadence-designer` when defending an email-first sequence (referral email Day 1, call only after reply or known stakeholder). Cited by `sales-researcher` as the origin of the SDR role specialization the founder will eventually face when scaling past 2 people.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite Predictable Revenue as evidence that "150-250 emails/week" is the right number for any specific business — the number is from one company in one era. Do not cite the role-split as the right structure for a 2-person team; at 2 people the founder IS the SDR and the closer and CSM, and the book's implicit assumption (you have hiring budget) is wrong for the elocal_clone bootstrap context. The Cold Calling 2.0 referral pattern is the most portable idea in the book; cite that.
