# Selling to Skeptical Trades

## One-line summary
Trades-vertical buyers — plumbers, electricians, HVAC techs, appliance repair owners — are the most-pitched and most-burned segment in B2B outbound; the patterns that work with them are different in kind from B2B SaaS patterns and reduce to four rules: trust before pitch, proof beats promise, founding-member framing for the bootstrappers, and recording-as-proof closes faster than any narrative.

## Why this tier
Scaffolding, not evidence. There is no published large-n study of cold-outbound to trades vertical. What exists is (a) the practitioner literature inside the elocal_clone knowledge base — `knowledge/05-contractor-acquisition.md` is the canonical internal source — (b) the founder's first-hand interviews with his plumber friend and his electrician friend (n=2, but n=2 of the actual ICP), (c) anecdotal pattern data from contractor-focused sales orgs (Service Titan's outbound team, JobNimbus, Housecall Pro). The patterns below are folded together from those sources and labeled scaffolding because they are forcing functions: a script that ignores them will fail in predictable ways, and a script that follows them will at least get the meeting. Whether they predict close rate at scale is unknown until the founder runs 200 dials and we have data.

## Key concepts
- **Trust is everything; trust is fragile; trust is built by specificity.** A trades buyer cannot evaluate your software demo, your founder credentials, or your VC backers — those signals are illegible to him. What he CAN evaluate: do you sound like you've actually been in his shop. Use trade-specific vocabulary (RBQ license in Quebec, ESA in Ontario, journeyman vs apprentice ratio, trip charge, callback rate, the difference between a service call and a job). Specificity = competence = trust.
- **Recording-as-proof closes faster than any pitch.** The single highest-leverage move in the elocal_clone playbook: play a real recording of an actual customer call from the morning of the cold call. Not a testimonial, not a case study — a 30-second clip of a real homeowner asking "can you come fix my hot water tank today?" The recording proves the leads are real in a way nothing else can, and it does it in 30 seconds. This is the load-bearing tactic; everything else in the script supports getting to the recording.
- **"Founding member" framing works with bootstrappers.** Trades owners who built their business from one truck respect the bootstrap mentality. "Founding member" framing — "you're one of the first 10 plumbers in Hamilton, you get the 10 free calls, you help us shape the product" — invokes Cialdini's Reciprocity AND Commitment AND Unity at once. The bootstrapper recognizes the underdog framing because he lived it.
- **No credit card today.** Trades owners have been burned by auto-renewal subscriptions (see `homeadvisor-angi-reverse-playbook.md`). Asking for a credit card on the first call kills 80% of conversions. The first ask is "let me send you 10 leads, no card needed, you tell me if they're real." Money is a Day 30 conversation, not a Day 0 conversation.
- **Time of day matters more than day of week.** Trades owners are unreachable 8am-5pm because they're on jobs. Best windows: 7-8am (before first job), 11:30-1pm (lunch break, between jobs), 5-6pm (back at the truck), and Saturday morning. The standard B2B SaaS "Tuesday 10am" rule does not apply.
- **The wife / family / partner gatekeeper.** In owner-operator trades businesses, the spouse often handles the books, the phone, and the "is this scam?" filter. The script needs to survive being summarized to a spouse who is even more skeptical than the owner. If the spouse can't recap it in a sentence, the deal is dead.
- **Reference contractors trump testimonials.** A trades owner does not trust a written testimonial. He trusts a phone call to "Bob, the plumber over in Stoney Creek who's been using this for 6 weeks." Build the script to offer the reference contractor by name and number, not as a logo on a slide.
- **Skepticism = engagement.** Do not flinch when the prospect goes hostile or accusatory. Hostile is engaged. The Voss Accusation Audit pattern works perfectly here — pre-emptively name the suspicion ("You're probably wondering if this is the same scam as HomeAdvisor — fair question, here's how we're different").

## When to cite
Cited by `script-writer` for **every script targeting a trades vertical** — the four rules above are the script's structural constraints. The recording-as-proof move is the script's spine; the founding-member framing is the script's emotional frame; the no-credit-card rule defines the CTA; the trade-vocabulary specificity defines the language register. Cited by `icp-analyst` when defining the trades ICP — the disqualification heuristics include "owner currently uses HomeAdvisor heavily" (high pain, high skepticism, high opportunity). Cited by `call-coach` when grading trades calls — the coach should specifically check whether the user used trade vocabulary, played the recording, and avoided asking for a card.

## Anti-citation guidance
Do not cite this file as if it were research — it is a synthesis of practitioner patterns and n=2 founder interviews. Once the founder has run 200+ real dials, this file should be updated with empirical findings and the tier may rise. Do not assume the patterns transfer to non-trades blue-collar (e.g., janitorial, landscaping, food trucks) without re-validating — the trades-specific vocabulary and licensing context is the load-bearing piece. Do not let `script-writer` use the recording-as-proof move without an actual recording — a fake or generic recording is worse than no recording.
